Common ground concepts:

Displacements and deformations of mass distributions are always caused by forces.

To address the effect of such forces we need to solve the equations of continuum mechanics

The force field transmitted to a volume element within a body by its surroundings (force per unit volume) is called stress.

Mechanics and buckling theory

General setting.








$\rho$: Mass density

$\mathbb{\sigma}$: Stress tensor

$\mathbb{F}=\nabla{\mathbb{\Phi}}$: Deformation gradient

This formalism applies to every continua for which a stress and a displacement fields can be defined.

The response of the material to stress should be imposed to distinguish a solid from other continua.

Living matter is well described by hyperelastic constitutive laws.

Mechanics: Buckling theory





Elements of every model.

1.- Thin sheet with bending with elastic modulus $E_f$ and thickness $h$

2.- Elastic foundation with elasticity modulus $E_s$

3.- Source of compression.


Buckling as a critical phenomena.

In general the problem at hand is to compute extremal values of the energy $U$

$$U=U_f+U_s-L$$

Where $U_f$ and $U_s$ represent the elastic energies of the substrate and the film.

$L$ represent any geometrical constraint (Lagrange multiplier) such as incompressibility.

A compressive threshold always needs to be reached in order to buckle.

Examples of compressive deformation.



Elastomer theory results.

Perturbation Theory:

$B_m\partial_l^4 y +F \partial_l ^2 y +Kq y +K_2 q^2 y^2=0$

This admits to solutions of the form:

$y=Acos(2\pi x/\lambda)$

If:

$F=\frac{4\pi^2B_m}{\lambda^2}+\frac{\lambda K}{2\pi}$

Which implies that if:

$F < F_c=3q_o^2B_m$

The solution is flat

If $F=F_c$ then one gets buckled solutions with:

$\lambda_o=2\pi \left( \frac{2B_m}{K} \right) ^{1/3} \sim h \left( \frac{E_f}{E_s} \right) ^ {1/3}$
